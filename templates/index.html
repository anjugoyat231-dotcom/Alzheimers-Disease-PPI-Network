About Alzheimer's Disease

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder and the most common cause of dementia, accounting for 60-80% of dementia cases worldwide. It is characterized by the accumulation of amyloid-beta plaques and hyperphosphorylated tau protein tangles in the brain, leading to progressive cognitive decline, memory loss, and functional impairment.

Key Genes in Alzheimer's Disease
Gene Full Name Role in AD
APP Amyloid Beta Precursor Protein Source of amyloid-beta peptides; central to plaque formation
PSEN1 Presenilin 1 Component of gamma-secretase complex; cleaves APP
PSEN2 Presenilin 2 Similar to PSEN1; mutations cause early-onset AD
APOE Apolipoprotein E Major genetic risk factor; APOE4 allele increases risk
MAPT Microtubule-Associated Protein Tau Forms neurofibrillary tangles; hyperphosphorylated in AD
BACE1 Beta-Secretase 1 Cleaves APP to initiate amyloid-beta production
BDNF Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor Supports neuronal survival; reduced in AD
GSK3B Glycogen Synthase Kinase 3 Beta Phosphorylates tau; involved in synaptic plasticity
Symptoms
  • Memory loss affecting daily life
  • Difficulty planning or solving problems
  • Confusion with time or place
  • Trouble understanding visual images
  • Problems with speaking or writing
  • Decreased judgment and decision-making
Biological Mechanisms
  • Amyloid Cascade: APP cleavage by BACE1 and gamma-secretase produces amyloid-beta oligomers
  • Tau Hyperphosphorylation: GSK3B and CDK5 hyperphosphorylate tau, forming tangles
  • Oxidative Stress: Mitochondrial dysfunction leads to ROS production
  • Neuroinflammation: Activated microglia release pro-inflammatory cytokines
  • Synaptic Dysfunction: Loss of synapses and impaired neurotransmission
  • Apoptosis: Programmed cell death of neurons
